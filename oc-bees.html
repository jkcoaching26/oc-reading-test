The Language of Bees

When a honeybee returns to its hive having found a rich source of nectar, it does not simply resume its place among the thousands of others clustered on the comb. Instead, it dances. The movements it performs — precise, repeatable, and apparently understood by its fellow workers — contain information about where the food is, how far away it lies, and how worth the journey it is. For decades, scientists refused to believe it.

The scientist who first decoded the bee's dance was an Austrian zoologist named Karl von Frisch. In the 1920s, while observing bees in his laboratory in Munich, von Frisch noticed that forager bees returning from a food source performed a distinctive figure-of-eight movement on the surface of the honeycomb. 12 ..................... Over the following decades, he devoted himself to decoding what the dance was communicating. By the time he published his conclusions in 1946, he had identified a system of extraordinary complexity — one that would eventually earn him the Nobel Prize.

13 ..................... Critics argued that a creature with a brain smaller than a sesame seed could not possibly encode and decode information of this kind. Von Frisch's supporters countered that the evidence was too consistent and too detailed to be dismissed. The debate continued for nearly two decades after his findings were first published. The waggle dance itself, meanwhile, had been described in precise detail. The dance appeared at first glance to be a simple, repetitive motion, consisting of a straight waggle run followed by a curved return loop to the starting point. 14 ..................... The duration of the waggle run indicates distance from the hive, measured not in metres but in units of time: a run lasting one second signals a food source approximately one kilometre away.

The dispute might have continued indefinitely had it not been for a new generation of researchers willing to test von Frisch's claims under controlled conditions. Sceptics continued to insist that bees were simply following familiar scents left by returning foragers, rather than reading any information from the dance itself. The debate grew sharp enough to attract attention beyond the scientific community. 15 ..................... In a series of carefully designed trials, researchers trained bees to visit food sources at precisely measured distances and in specific directions, then released naïve bees — those with no previous knowledge of the location — and observed whether they flew to the correct site. In study after study, they did.

Across more than five decades of research since the controversy was resolved, the findings have revealed a system of considerably greater sophistication than even von Frisch had imagined. 16 ..................... One colony, studied by Dr Mandyam Srinivasan at the Australian National University, was found to use a waggle run duration approximately fifteen per cent shorter than European colonies signalling an equivalent distance — a difference consistent enough to suggest that bees in different regions have, over generations, calibrated their dance to local conditions. The researchers noted that this finding was among the most unexpected of the study. 17 .....................

The bee, it now seems, does not simply react to its environment. It describes that environment to others, in a language specific enough to convey compass bearing, distance, and relative value. Whether this constitutes language in any meaningful sense remains a question scientists continue to debate. The bees, for their part, appear indifferent to the controversy.
