The City That Makes Its Own Weather

If you have ever noticed that city centres feel hotter than the surrounding countryside on a still summer evening, you have experienced a phenomenon that scientists have been studying for decades. Cities are warmer than the rural areas around them — sometimes by as much as five degrees Celsius — and this difference is not simply a result of human activity generating heat. The built environment itself plays a surprisingly active role in trapping and retaining warmth in ways that have far-reaching consequences.

Unlike soil or grass, the materials used to construct roads, pavements and buildings absorb large quantities of heat from the sun during daylight hours. 15 ..................... As a result, temperatures in the urban core remain elevated long after sunset, creating conditions that prevent the natural cooling that rural areas experience overnight.

This sustained warmth has an unexpected effect on local weather patterns. Hot air rises rapidly above city centres, creating a column of ascending air over the densest parts of the built environment. 16 ..................... This incoming moisture, lifted quickly to higher and cooler parts of the atmosphere, condenses and falls as rain — often directly over the city itself, rather than across the open land nearby.

The result is that many large cities receive considerably more rainfall than the countryside immediately around them, and the difference is not trivial. 17 ..................... Scientists describe this pattern as urban-induced precipitation, and it has now been recorded in cities across Europe, Asia and North America, suggesting it is a consequence of urban design itself rather than regional climate.

The increased rainfall, however, does not behave the way rain does in the countryside. In natural environments, much of the water that falls is absorbed gradually by soil and released slowly into waterways. In cities, water races across sealed surfaces rather than soaking into the ground. 18 ..................... This is why flash flooding has become a growing problem in urban areas worldwide, even in cities where the total amount of annual rainfall has not significantly increased.

In response, many city governments have begun promoting green roofs, tree-lined streets and permeable pavements as ways of reducing the urban heat island effect. 19 ..................... Critics argue that the fundamental issue lies in the way cities have been designed from the outset, and that surface-level measures cannot address problems built into the very structure of urban areas. A growing number of planners are therefore calling for wholesale changes to the way new cities are built. 20 ..................... Whether governments will act on such proposals quickly enough to make a meaningful difference remains, for now, an open question.
