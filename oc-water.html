Extract A

The Romans did not invent the aqueduct, but they perfected it. By the first century CE, eleven major aqueducts supplied the city of Rome with an estimated one million cubic metres of water each day — enough, by modern calculations, for a population of several million people. This was not merely an engineering achievement but a social one: the availability of clean water in public fountains, bathhouses, and private homes reorganised the texture of daily life, making possible levels of urban density, public health, and civic organisation that no previous civilisation had sustained. Cities that controlled reliable water sources grew; those that did not remained small or disappeared entirely.

The lesson of Rome was not immediately absorbed by the civilisations that followed it. For much of medieval Europe, water was drawn from rivers and wells that were routinely contaminated by the same communities that depended on them, and epidemic disease was a near-constant feature of urban life. It was only in the nineteenth century, when the connection between contaminated water and cholera was finally established by the physician John Snow, that European cities began the systematic overhaul of their water and sewage infrastructure that would, within a generation, transform urban mortality rates and make large, densely populated cities survivable for ordinary people. The infrastructure that now delivers clean water to homes across Australia is the direct descendant of those nineteenth-century decisions — a system so reliable that most of us engage with it without a second thought.

Extract B

The second summer without rain, the paddocks on our property stopped looking like land and started looking like a decision that had gone wrong. The stock dam was down to a dark band of water at the centre, surrounded by a widening ring of cracked grey mud that had set hard in the heat, each crack running so deep and straight it looked deliberate — as though the earth had drawn lines to mark how far it intended to go. We were carting water by then. My father had stopped talking about when it would break and had started talking about what we would do if it didn't.

Our neighbour from the property to the west came over one afternoon without being asked. She had farmed through three droughts before ours and had the particular steadiness of someone who has already survived the thing you are afraid of. She did not say it would be all right. She looked at the dam, looked at the sky, asked one or two practical questions about the stock, and then had a cup of tea at the kitchen table before driving home. I did not fully understand, at the time, why her visit helped as much as it did. It was only later, when I had my own accumulation of difficult seasons behind me, that I understood water differently — not as something I was owed, but as something that came and went on terms I had no authority over.

Extract C

The total amount of water on Earth has remained essentially constant for billions of years. What changes is its location and form, as it moves continuously between the atmosphere, the land surface, and the subsurface in the process that hydrologists call the 'hydrological cycle.' Water evaporates from oceans and other surface bodies, rises into the atmosphere, condenses into clouds, and falls as precipitation. On land, some of this water flows across the surface into rivers and lakes, while the remainder filters through the soil in a process known as 'percolation,' eventually reaching layers of permeable rock known as 'aquifers' that can store water for decades, centuries, or longer.

The movement of water from soil and vegetation back into the atmosphere involves a combined process that hydrologists term 'evapotranspiration' — a single measure that accounts for both direct evaporation from the ground surface and the release of water through the leaves of plants. The area of land that drains into a particular river system is called a 'watershed' or 'catchment', and the depth below the surface at which the ground becomes permanently saturated is known as the 'water table.' Understanding these relationships is central to the management of water resources, particularly in regions where rainfall is irregular or aquifer recharge rates are slower than the rates at which groundwater is being extracted for agricultural or urban use.

Extract D

For Aboriginal Australians, water has never been simply a resource. In many language groups and traditions, significant water sources — springs, soakwaters, rock holes, and rivers — are understood as living elements of Country: connected to ancestral beings, encoded in songlines, and carrying obligations of care that extend across generations. A rock hole is not merely a place where water collects; it is a site of spiritual significance, a point in a network of relationships between people, animals, plants, and the land itself, and a responsibility that belongs to particular custodians whose duty it is to maintain the knowledge of its location, its seasonal behaviour, and the protocols governing its use. To know where water is — and when, and why — was among the most important forms of knowledge a person could hold.

This understanding shaped not only how Aboriginal communities moved across the landscape but how they perceived their relationship to it. The deep knowledge of water sources, accumulated across tens of thousands of years of careful observation and encoded in oral tradition, ceremony, and story, allowed communities to live sustainably in environments that European settlers, without that knowledge, found impossibly harsh. Many early colonial expeditions that ended in disaster did so because their members lacked the understanding of water that local Aboriginal people carried. In recent years, there has been a growing recognition among land managers, ecologists, and water authorities that this knowledge — of where water lies, how it moves, and what it depends on — represents a form of ecological intelligence that contemporary water management has much to learn from.
