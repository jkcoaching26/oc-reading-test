The Discovery of Penicillin

Few scientific discoveries have changed the world as quickly or as dramatically as the discovery of penicillin. Before its arrival, infections that today seem minor — a cut, a burst appendix, a bout of pneumonia — were frequently fatal. The discovery that a common mould could kill the bacteria responsible for such infections did not come from a single moment of genius. It came, instead, from a long chain of accidents, missed opportunities, and persistent hard work.

By the early twentieth century, scientists already knew that microorganisms caused infectious disease, but they had no reliable means of fighting them once they entered the body. Surgeons relied on antiseptics that often damaged human tissue as readily as they killed bacteria. Physicians could offer their patients little more than rest and the hope that the body's own defences would prove sufficient. The search for a better solution had been underway for decades.

The breakthrough that would eventually become penicillin began in September 1928, when the Scottish bacteriologist Alexander Fleming noticed something unexpected on one of his culture plates. 12 The paper he eventually produced attracted little attention from the medical community, and Fleming moved on to other research. The explanation for what he had seen only emerged later. 13 A spore of Penicillium mould had drifted in through an open window and landed on the dish. Where the mould grew, it appeared to be killing the bacteria immediately around it. 14 That question would take twelve more years and two other scientists to answer.

In 1938, the chemist Ernst Chain and the pathologist Howard Florey, both working at Oxford University, came across Fleming's overlooked paper and recognised its potential. Over the following two years, they developed a method for purifying penicillin and testing it systematically. In 1940, they injected eight mice with a lethal dose of Streptococcus bacteria. Four were then given doses of penicillin; four received nothing. By the following morning, all four untreated mice had died. All four treated mice had survived. 15 With funding secured from both the British and American governments, the team began producing the drug in industrial quantities.

The Oxford clinical trials began in 1941, treating patients whose conditions had been considered entirely beyond the reach of medicine. 16 One of the first, a police officer named Albert Alexander, had developed a life-threatening infection from a small scratch on his face. Within twenty-four hours of receiving penicillin, his fever had dropped and the infection had begun to retreat. By the end of the war, the drug was being produced in quantities sufficient to treat every Allied casualty, and it is estimated to have saved more than fifty million lives in the decades that followed. 17

Fleming, Chain and Florey were jointly awarded the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine in 1945. Fleming, typically understated, described his role as little more than noticing something that others had overlooked. What he had noticed, of course, had changed everything.
