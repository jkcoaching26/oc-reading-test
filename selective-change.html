Extract A

My father retired on a Friday in March, after forty-one years with the same employer. We had expected, as a family, that he would be glad. The work had been difficult for years — the commute long, the management changed, the role quietly diminished by reorganisations he had not been consulted about. When the date was finally set, we spoke of it the way people speak of a recovery. He would have time, now. He could rest.

What we had not anticipated was the silence that followed. Not a peaceful silence — a hollow one. He moved through the first weeks as though the days had lost their shape. He rose at the same hour, dressed with the same care, ate at the same times. But the structure he had moved inside for four decades was gone, and what remained was its outline: habits continuing without the thing they had been built around.

It was only much later, watching him gradually reassemble a life around smaller routines, that I understood what I had missed. My father had not simply resisted change throughout his career — he had organised his whole sense of himself around the ability to remain constant. The resistance had not been stubbornness. It had been identity. And when the change he had held off finally arrived, it did not feel like relief. It felt like the removal of the one thing that had defined him.

Extract B

The brain does not experience change. It experiences the gap between what it predicted and what actually happened. This is not a semantic distinction. The predictive processing model — one of the most influential frameworks in contemporary neuroscience — holds that the brain is not a passive receiver of information but a prediction machine, constantly generating expectations about what will come next and updating those expectations when reality diverges. Change, on this account, is not a feature of the world. It is a signal that the brain's model of the world needs revision.

This has consequences that are easy to overlook. It means that even change a person has actively chosen can feel like loss — not because the change itself is unwanted, but because the brain's existing prediction model has been made suddenly unreliable. The body of habits, expectations and assumptions built up around a job, a relationship or a home does not dissolve when the situation changes. It persists, generating predictions that no longer apply, producing a low-level disorientation that is often mistaken for grief.

There is, however, a more hopeful implication. Because the prediction model is built from experience, it can be rebuilt. People who undergo frequent change do not simply endure it better — they develop more flexible prediction models, capable of updating more quickly when reality diverges from expectation. Adaptability, on this account, is not a fixed trait. It is a capacity that repeated exposure to change can, over time, produce.

Extract C

Change, in our time, is treated as self-evidently good. Businesses are praised for disruption, cities for reinvention, individuals for pivoting. The language of innovation runs through almost every public conversation about the future — in politics, in technology, in education. To resist change is to be on the wrong side of history. To embrace it is to be forward-thinking, adaptive, alive.

What this framing tends to leave out is the question of distribution. Change is rarely experienced equally. A restructuring that increases a company's efficiency may also eliminate jobs that took years to secure. A neighbourhood transformed by investment may become unrecognisable to those who built their lives there. Urban renewal, policy reform, technological disruption — in each case, the benefits tend to flow toward those already in a position to receive them, while the costs tend to fall on those least equipped to absorb them.

What is most striking about the enthusiasm for change is how consistently it is expressed by those with the least to lose. The loudest advocates for disruption rarely inhabit the roles most vulnerable to being disrupted. The people who design the changes and the people who live inside them are often not the same people. This does not make change wrong. It makes the celebration of it, in the abstract, a rather comfortable position to hold.

Extract D

One of the habits that most reliably misleads us about change is the assumption that it happens at the moment it becomes visible. Political revolutions, scientific breakthroughs, social transformations — these are routinely described as sudden events, the products of particular moments in which everything altered. Historians who study these processes closely tend to tell a different story. What looks like rupture from outside is almost always, on examination, the final stage of a much longer preparation.

The abolition of practices once considered permanent, the adoption of ideas once considered unthinkable — these rarely emerge fully formed. They accumulate. Conditions shift, arguments develop, small precedents are set, individuals act in ways that seem minor at the time. The moment that appears decisive is usually the moment at which change became impossible to ignore, not the moment at which it began. Understanding this requires a different relationship to historical evidence: less attention to dramatic events, more attention to the quiet processes that made them possible.

This reframing matters for how we think about the present. If most change is already under way before it is noticed, then the most useful question to ask is not 'when did this change?' but 'when did it begin to change?' — and the answer is almost always earlier than expected. Watching for the early stages of change, rather than waiting for its arrival, produces a different understanding of where we are and what may be coming.
