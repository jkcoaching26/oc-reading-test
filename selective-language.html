Extract A

My parents spoke two languages at home until I was about seven, and then, gradually, they stopped. There was no decision, no announcement — just a slow drift toward the easier option. English was the language of school, of friends, of everything outside the front door. The other language retreated. By the time I was twelve, I understood it but could no longer produce it with any confidence.

What I did not understand until much later was that the two languages had never felt equal. English was the language I used to think. The other was the language I used to perform — at my grandparents' house, at certain festivals, in situations where it was expected. This distinction felt natural for years. It was only when I began to study linguistics as an adult that I recognised it as a form of loss, though a particular kind: not the loss of access, but the loss of intimacy. The language was still there. It simply no longer felt like mine.

Stranger still, I later discovered that the language had not gone as quiet inside me as I had assumed. In dreams, it surfaced without warning. Under strong emotion — grief, startlement, sudden joy — words arrived in that language first, before English had a chance to organise itself. Whatever had been pushed underground had not disappeared. It had simply been waiting.

Extract B

Languages do not merely describe the world. They determine what parts of it you are able to see. This is not a metaphor. Research into what linguists call linguistic relativity has produced a body of evidence that is difficult to dismiss: the language you speak shapes, in measurable ways, how you perceive colour, how you orient yourself in space, and how you experience the passage of time.

Speakers of languages with many distinct colour terms reliably distinguish shades that speakers of languages with fewer terms struggle to separate, even when looking at identical stimuli. Communities whose languages use cardinal directions — north, south, east, west — rather than relative terms like left and right maintain a constant, precise sense of orientation that speakers of egocentric languages find difficult to replicate. The differences are not trivial. They are structural.

If this is true, then the death of a language is not merely a cultural loss, as it is often framed. It is the permanent disappearance of a distinct cognitive orientation — a particular way of organising experience that no other language can fully replicate. Of the approximately seven thousand languages spoken in the world today, more than half are expected to fall silent within this century. Each one will take its own version of the world with it.

Extract C

English is, by most measures, the closest thing the world has ever had to a common language. It is the language of international science, global finance, and the internet. For non-native speakers who achieve fluency, it represents access — to education, to opportunity, to conversations that would otherwise be closed. None of this is trivial. The pragmatic case for English is, on its own terms, overwhelming.

What is less often acknowledged is the cost this dominance imposes — not only on speakers of minority languages, who must invest years acquiring a tongue that is not theirs, but on native English speakers themselves. Monolingualism, research increasingly suggests, is a cognitive narrowing. The effort of moving between two languages — of reaching for a word that does not map neatly onto its equivalent, of thinking in a structure that forces different distinctions — exercises the mind in ways that never having to do so does not. English speakers, having inherited fluency as a birthright, are frequently spared this friction. That ease, it turns out, may come at a price.

There is something quietly ironic about this. The language that has come to dominate global communication may be producing, in its native speakers, a subtle disadvantage: the very smoothness of their English makes the discomfort of genuine translation easy to avoid. They can get by without it. Whether 'getting by' and 'thinking well' are the same thing is a question worth sitting with.

Extract D

The linguist Noam Chomsky proposed in the 1960s that human beings are born with an innate capacity for language — a set of structural principles so fundamental that they underlie every known human tongue. What varies between languages, on this account, is surface detail. What stays constant is the deep architecture. Children, Chomsky argued, could not possibly acquire language from exposure alone without some built-in framework to guide them. The speed and accuracy with which they do so seemed to require it.

Subsequent research has both complicated and supported this picture. What has proven difficult to challenge is the role of timing. Children who are exposed to a language before the age of roughly seven acquire it, given sufficient input, with near-native fluency and without formal instruction. The same children, exposed to the same language a decade later, work harder and achieve less. The window — often called the critical period — is not a myth. It is one of the most robustly replicated findings in the study of how minds acquire language.

What happens after the window closes is, in its own way, equally interesting. Adult learners do not simply perform a slower version of what children do. Brain imaging studies show that late learners activate different neural regions when processing their second language — regions associated with conscious effort and problem-solving rather than the automatic, intuitive processing that characterises native-language use. They are not failing to do what children do. They are doing something else entirely.
