The Early Struggles of Flying Machines

For most of recorded history, human flight existed only in myth. The story of Icarus warned against the ambition; the reality proved far more stubborn. Across two centuries, inventors drew blueprints, built contraptions and launched themselves from hills and towers, only to fall. Yet each failure carried within it something useful: a small correction, a new question, an insight that the next generation would quietly inherit. The history of aviation is not a story of sudden genius. It is a story of accumulated failure.

The foundations of modern aviation were laid not by an aviator but by a Yorkshire landowner. Sir George Cayley spent decades studying the science of lift and drag, publishing a paper in 1809 that would define the principles on which all future aircraft were based. Cayley was unusual among the inventors of his era in one crucial respect. 12 In 1853, his coachman became the first person to cross a valley in a heavier-than-air glider — though, by all accounts, the coachman promptly resigned.

13 In the 1840s, William Henson and his business partner John Stringfellow became convinced that steam power offered the answer. Henson designed the Aerial Steam Carriage — a machine on paper featuring fixed wings spanning nearly 45 metres and a steam engine to drive two propellers. Stringfellow built a scaled-down version and tested it in an empty factory. 14 The public, however, had already lost interest; without private backing, the project quietly collapsed.

Otto Lilienthal was the first person to achieve controlled gliding flight with any regularity. Between 1891 and 1896, he completed more than two thousand flights from a hill he had constructed outside Berlin, carefully shifting his body weight to steer. Lilienthal understood that the knowledge required to fly safely could only be built through patient, systematic observation. 15 Yet for all his precision, the sky offered no guarantees. In 1896, his glider stalled in a gust of wind and he fell from a height of fifteen metres. He died the following day. His final recorded words were: 'Sacrifices must be made.'

The task that had defeated so many others was finally completed by two brothers from Ohio who had never attended university. 16 Orville Wright made the first powered, controlled flight in history at Kitty Hawk, North Carolina, on 17 December 1903. The Flyer stayed in the air for twelve seconds and covered thirty-seven metres. By the end of the day, the brothers had completed four flights, the longest lasting nearly a minute. 17

Within a decade, aircraft were crossing the English Channel, carrying passengers and, before long, changing the nature of warfare. The dream that had consumed and occasionally killed the inventors of two centuries had become, quite suddenly, an industry.
