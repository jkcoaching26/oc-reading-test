Extract A

The ancient Olympic Games, held at Olympia in Greece every four years from at least 776 BCE, were far more than athletic competitions. They were religious ceremonies, diplomatic occasions, and a mechanism for achieving temporary peace between city-states that spent much of the rest of their time at war. Athletes competed not for money but for the olive wreath — a symbol of honour so highly prized that victors were celebrated as heroes and, in some cities, exempted from paying taxes for life. The Games were abolished by the Roman Emperor Theodosius I in 393 CE, partly on the grounds that they had become associated with pagan worship in an increasingly Christian empire.

It was the French educator Pierre de Coubertin who argued, in the 1890s, that reviving the Olympic Games could serve a practical purpose in the modern world: he believed competitive sport between nations would encourage understanding, reduce the likelihood of conflict, and promote physical education in schools. The first modern Games were held in Athens in 1896. Within a century, the Olympics had grown from a small gathering of mainly European amateurs into a global event involving thousands of athletes from over two hundred nations, generating billions of dollars in television rights, sponsorship, and tourism revenue. Whether this transformation has fulfilled de Coubertin's original vision, or fundamentally contradicted it, remains a matter of genuine debate among historians of sport.

Extract B

I was eleven years old the first time I stood behind the blocks at a proper competition. The pool looked nothing like the one I trained in every morning: it was longer, brighter, and full of a silence that pressed against my ears like water already. My coach, a compact woman of very few words, appeared at my elbow without warning. 'You're going to want to think about the first fifteen metres,' she said. 'After that, your body knows what to do. Just get to fifteen metres.' I nodded as though this made sense. It did not, yet.

The starter's signal sounded and I hit the water. For the first few strokes, my mind was entirely empty — not calm, just blank, the way a room looks before the furniture arrives. Then, somewhere around the ten-metre mark, something clicked into place: my breathing found its rhythm, my arms stopped fighting the water and started moving through it, and the rest of the race became, if not easy, then at least familiar. I touched the wall and looked up at the clock. I had not won. But my coach was nodding slowly from the poolside, which I had learned, over two years of training, was the closest she came to applause. I went back the following week, and the week after that.

Extract C

When the body engages in intense physical activity, its demand for energy outpaces the rate at which oxygen can be delivered to working muscles. In response, the muscles switch from aerobic respiration — which requires oxygen — to anaerobic respiration, which does not. This process generates energy rapidly but produces lactic acid as a by-product. The accumulation of lactic acid in muscle tissue is associated with the burning sensation athletes commonly experience during maximal effort, and with the temporary reduction in muscular force that follows. The point at which the body begins to rely significantly on anaerobic respiration is known among exercise physiologists as the 'lactate threshold'.

An athlete's capacity for sustained high-intensity effort is also determined by their maximum oxygen uptake, referred to in sports science as 'VO₂ max': the greatest volume of oxygen the body can consume per minute during exercise. VO₂ max is partly genetic but can be improved through targeted training. At the muscular level, performance is further shaped by the ratio of 'fast-twitch' to 'slow-twitch' muscle fibres — terms used by exercise scientists to distinguish between fibres optimised for explosive short-duration efforts and those suited to endurance activity. Elite sprinters tend to have a higher proportion of fast-twitch fibres, while distance runners typically show the reverse pattern. Coaches and sports scientists use this understanding to design training programmes that target the specific physiological demands of each event.

Extract D

The use of performance-enhancing substances in competitive sport is governed in Australia by the National Anti-Doping Scheme, administered by the Australian Sports Anti-Doping Authority, commonly known as ASADA. Under this scheme, athletes competing at national and international level are subject to testing both in and out of competition, and are required to comply with the World Anti-Doping Agency's Prohibited List, which is updated annually. An athlete who tests positive for a prohibited substance, or who is found to have evaded testing, may face a period of ineligibility ranging from a reprimand to a lifetime ban, depending on the substance involved and the circumstances of the case.

The rationale behind anti-doping regulation is not only to protect the health of individual athletes but to preserve what regulators describe as the 'spirit of sport': the principle that competition should reflect natural human ability, dedication, and training rather than access to chemical advantage. Critics of the current framework argue that enforcement is inconsistent across different sports and national systems, and that the burden of proof placed on athletes to demonstrate innocence — rather than on authorities to prove intent — creates a system that can produce unjust outcomes. Several high-profile cases in recent years have prompted calls for an independent review of how sanctions are determined and applied.
