The Story of Salt

It sits in small ceramic dishes on restaurant tables and pours freely from plastic shakers in school canteens. Salt is so ordinary today that it is difficult to imagine it once shaped the destinies of nations. Yet for most of human history, salt was among the most strategically important substances on Earth. Governments taxed it, wars were fought over it, and entire civilisations organised themselves around their ability to obtain it.

Salt's extraordinary value arose from a single, irreplaceable function. Before refrigeration, there was no reliable method of keeping food from spoiling. 12 ..................... The importance of salt was so widely understood that Roman soldiers were sometimes paid in it, a practice that gave rise to the English word 'salary'. In medieval Europe, a household's supply of salt was considered as essential as its supply of firewood or grain.

The power that salt gave to governments was perhaps nowhere more nakedly displayed than in France. The gabelle — a tax on salt — was introduced by King Philip VI in 1341 and would remain in force, in various forms, for more than four hundred years. It required every French subject over the age of eight to purchase a fixed quantity of salt each year from government warehouses at a price set by the crown. 13 ..................... By the sixteenth century, however, the gabelle had become a permanent fixture of French life, generating more than a third of the crown's annual income. Salt inspectors were appointed to enforce compliance, and those caught smuggling untaxed salt faced punishment that could include forced labour in the royal galleys. The gabelle was widely considered among the most unjust of France's many taxes and was cited repeatedly in the list of grievances presented to the king at the start of the French Revolution. 14 .....................

Salt's importance extended well beyond the practical. In cultures across the world, it carried a deep symbolic weight. At formal feasts in ancient Rome, salt was placed at the centre of the table as a mark of hospitality; guests seated closest to it were considered the most honoured. In parts of the Middle East, sharing bread and salt with a visitor was understood as a guarantee of protection that lasted for three days. The English phrase 'worth one's salt' — meaning capable and deserving of one's wages — survives as a trace of this older world, in which salt and a person's value were genuinely connected. 15 ..................... Salt merchants who controlled the key production sites accumulated extraordinary fortunes. In some regions, they became more powerful than the local nobility, and their influence over food supply gave them a political leverage that few rulers could afford to ignore.

The need to transport salt over long distances gave rise to some of the ancient world's most significant trade routes. Roads were built, sea lanes were established, and caravans crossed deserts specifically to move salt from the places where it could be extracted to the places where it was needed. 16 ..................... One of the most powerful of these, the city of Venice, built much of its early prosperity on its control of the salt trade across the Adriatic Sea. At its height, Venice maintained salt depots in ports from Egypt to England, and the income from these operations funded the construction of the palaces and churches that still draw visitors to the city today. 17 .....................

Today, salt costs almost nothing and spoilage is solved by electricity rather than minerals. The substance that once built cities and brought down governments now fills a small white packet on a café table. Its extraordinary history is all but invisible, hidden in plain sight.
