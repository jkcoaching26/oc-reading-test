The Secret Life of Elephants

Of all the animals that share the African savannah, none has attracted more scientific fascination than the elephant. For most of human history, elephants were admired chiefly for their size and strength. That admiration has since deepened considerably. Over the past fifty years, researchers studying elephant behaviour have uncovered evidence of capabilities that challenge some of our most fundamental assumptions about animal intelligence. The elephant, it now appears, is a far more complex creature than anyone had previously imagined.

Elephants live in family groups led by a single older female, known as the matriarch, whose experience and memory are the group's most important resource. In one remarkable case documented in Kenya, a matriarch led her herd more than two hundred kilometres across arid land to a waterhole that had been dry for over thirty years. The journey appeared to have been entirely deliberate. 12 Researchers who studied the case concluded that the ability to store and transmit geographical knowledge across generations may be one of the most critical survival advantages elephants possess.

13 Scientists studying elephant herds in Kenya's Amboseli Basin made a striking discovery in the 1980s: elephants were communicating using sounds pitched far below the range of human hearing. These infrasonic calls, produced as vibrations in the elephant's forehead, can travel through the ground as well as the air, allowing elephants to coordinate with others several kilometres away. When researchers began systematically recording these calls, they identified more than seventy distinct vocalisations, each associated with a particular social context. 14

Elephants are among the very few animals known to display what appears to be grief. When a member of a herd dies, surviving elephants have been observed standing over the body for hours, touching it gently with their trunks, and returning to the site repeatedly over subsequent months and years. 15 Dr Cynthia Moss, who spent more than forty years observing elephants in Amboseli, documented one family that returned annually to the exact spot where their matriarch had died, even after the bones had been removed and relocated by researchers.

Across dozens of studies conducted over four decades, the findings have revealed striking variations between elephant families. 16 One female in particular, named Echo and studied by Dr Moss over a period of thirty years, demonstrated what the research team described as an almost deliberate tenderness when encountering the bones of deceased relatives, using her trunk to position each bone carefully before moving on. 17

For a long time, intelligence was assumed to require a large brain and a complex social structure. Elephants have neither, by the standards that once seemed to matter. What they have instead, it appears, is something considerably more difficult to measure: the capacity to think.
