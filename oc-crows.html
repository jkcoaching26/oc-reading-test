The Intelligence of Crows

Of all the birds that share our cities and countryside, few have attracted more scientific attention than the crow. For centuries, crows were regarded as pests — noisy, opportunistic, and not especially interesting. That view has changed dramatically. Over the past three decades, researchers studying crow behaviour have produced findings that have surprised even experienced biologists. The crow, it turns out, is one of the most cognitively capable animals on the planet.

Crows are among the very few non-human animals known to make and use tools. In parts of Japan, carrion crows have been observed dropping hard-shelled nuts onto pedestrian crossings and waiting for passing cars to crack them open. In New Caledonia, crows fashion hooked sticks from twigs to extract insects from narrow crevices in bark. In a laboratory experiment conducted at Oxford University, a researcher presented a group of crows with a transparent tube of water containing a floating piece of food just out of reach. The crows dropped stones into the tube, raising the water level until the food was within reach. 12 The experiment has since been replicated with wild crows across several continents, consistently producing the same result.

13 Building on this evidence, researchers at the University of Auckland designed a series of experiments to investigate how crows approach problems they have never encountered before. The most revealing of these became known as the Aesop's Fable task: a transparent tube of water, a floating reward just out of reach, and a selection of objects the crow could choose to drop in. 14 When researchers introduced objects of different weights and sizes, the crows consistently selected those most likely to displace the greatest volume of water, demonstrating not just memory but active physical reasoning.

Tool use is not the only area in which crows have surprised researchers. Studies at the University of Washington found that crows can recognise individual human faces and remember them for years. In one experiment, researchers wore distinctive masks while trapping and releasing crows on campus. In the weeks that followed, crows that had been trapped began scolding anyone wearing the same mask, even when the mask was worn by someone the birds had never encountered before. Crows that had not been trapped ignored the mask entirely. 15 The implications for our understanding of animal cognition are, researchers argue, considerable.

Across more than forty published studies conducted over the past two decades, the results have been striking in their diversity. 16 One crow in particular, observed by Dr Sarah Jelbert over three consecutive field seasons in New Caledonia, solved every new variant of the Aesop task presented to her — including versions that required her to ignore a tempting but ineffective object placed beside the correct one. The researchers noted that she appeared to inspect each object carefully before making her selection, pausing in a manner that several members of the team described as unmistakably deliberate. 17 For a long time, intelligence was assumed to require a large brain and a complex social structure. Crows have neither, by the standards that once seemed to matter. What they have instead, it appears, is something considerably more difficult to measure: the capacity to think.
