Extract A

Before the railway, most people in Britain lived and died within a few miles of where they were born. The pace of travel was limited to the speed of a horse, which meant that a journey from London to Edinburgh took several days and cost more than many working people earned in a month. The opening of the Liverpool and Manchester Railway in 1830 — the world's first inter-city passenger service — began a process of change so rapid that contemporaries struggled to find words for it. Within thirty years, over ten thousand miles of track had been laid across Britain, and the country had been fundamentally remade.

The social consequences of this transformation were profound. For the first time, it became possible for large numbers of working people to live at a distance from their place of employment, giving rise to the commuter suburbs that would define the shape of modern cities. Fresh food could be transported from rural areas to urban markets before it spoiled, improving diet across the population. Newspapers printed in London could reach Edinburgh by the following morning, creating for the first time something approaching a shared national conversation. Historians of technology have argued that no single innovation before or since has done more to compress the experience of distance, and with it, to reshape the expectations of ordinary life.

Extract B

The train stopped somewhere between Armidale and Tamworth and did not move again for three hours. No announcement was made. Outside the window, the paddocks sat in the afternoon heat with the absolute stillness of things that have given up waiting, and the carriage gradually filled with the particular silence of people who have run out of things to pretend to do on their phones. I had a connecting bus to catch at five o'clock. By half past three, I had accepted that I would not be catching it.

The woman in the seat across the aisle had been watching me recalculate my options with what I can only describe as professional sympathy. She had the look of someone who has been delayed on this line many times before and has made her peace with it. 'There's a hotel above the pub on the main street,' she said, without looking up from her book. 'Tell them Sandra sent you. They'll sort you out.' She said it the way people give directions — matter-of-factly, as though the information had been waiting there for someone to need it. I did not ask how she knew where I was going. I simply wrote down the name of the pub. It was, in the end, a perfectly good evening.

Extract C

The ability of an aircraft to leave the ground depends on four physical forces acting simultaneously: lift, weight, thrust, and drag. Lift is generated by the shape of the wing, known technically as an 'aerofoil': the upper surface of the wing is curved so that air travelling over it must cover a greater distance than air passing beneath, causing it to move faster. According to Bernoulli's principle, faster-moving air exerts less pressure, creating a pressure difference between the upper and lower surfaces of the wing that pushes the aircraft upward. For lift to exceed the aircraft's weight, the engines must produce sufficient thrust to overcome the resistance of the air — a force engineers call 'drag.'

Aeronautical engineers distinguish between several types of drag. 'Parasite drag' is caused by the overall shape of the aircraft moving through air and increases with speed. 'Induced drag' is a by-product of lift itself: as the wing generates lift, it also creates vortices at its tips that reduce efficiency. Engineers minimise induced drag through careful wing design, including the use of 'winglets' — the upturned tips visible on most modern commercial aircraft. The behaviour of air flowing smoothly over a surface without breaking into turbulence is described as 'laminar flow', and maintaining it across as much of the wing as possible is a central goal of aeronautical design. When the angle at which the wing meets oncoming air becomes too steep, laminar flow breaks down entirely in a phenomenon known as a 'stall.'

Extract D

Road trauma is among the leading causes of preventable death and serious injury in Australia, and the legislative response to this has evolved considerably over the past century. Speed limits were introduced in most Australian states during the early twentieth century, initially at levels that reflected the capabilities of vehicles of the time, and have been progressively refined as research into crash dynamics has improved. The mandatory wearing of seatbelts, introduced in Victoria in 1970 and subsequently adopted across all Australian states and territories, is estimated to have prevented tens of thousands of deaths and is now recognised internationally as one of the most effective road safety interventions ever implemented.

Blood alcohol concentration limits for drivers were established to address the well-documented relationship between alcohol consumption and impaired reaction time, reduced hazard perception, and increased crash risk. The current limit for fully licensed drivers in Australia is 0.05 grams of alcohol per 100 millilitres of blood, one of the stricter standards in the developed world. Random breath testing, introduced in New South Wales in 1982, has been credited with producing a sustained reduction in alcohol-related road fatalities. The graduated licensing system — under which new drivers face additional restrictions on passengers, night driving, and speed — reflects evidence that inexperience is itself a significant risk factor, particularly during the first year of independent driving.
