Extract A

The fire that broke out in a bakery on Pudding Lane in the early hours of 2 September 1666 burned for four days and destroyed more than thirteen thousand houses, eighty-seven churches, and the original St Paul's Cathedral. The loss of life was surprisingly small, but the destruction of property was so vast that an estimated one hundred thousand people were left homeless in a city of perhaps five hundred thousand. What made the fire spread so rapidly was the nature of the city itself: the medieval buildings of London were almost entirely built of timber, often with upper storeys that jutted out over the narrow streets, creating an almost continuous canopy of dry wood through which the flames moved with frightening speed.

The rebuilding that followed transformed not only the physical fabric of London but the legal and commercial landscape of England. The Rebuilding Act of 1667 required that all new structures be erected in brick or stone, establishing for the first time a set of enforceable standards governing what could be built and where. Within a few years of the fire, the world's first property insurance companies had been founded in London, created specifically in response to the vulnerability that the disaster had exposed. Historians of urban development regard the Great Fire as one of the pivotal moments in the emergence of modern city planning: it was the catastrophe that forced a medieval city to reimagine itself as something more durable.

Extract B

Three days of watching the alerts had not prepared me for the afternoon the light turned strange. I had assumed, in the abstract way you assume things you hope will never happen, that I would know what to do when the time came. Instead I found myself standing in the middle of the kitchen with a bag I had started to pack and then abandoned, uncertain whether the things I had chosen made sense, uncertain whether leaving made sense, uncertain about most things.

The smoke had been building from the north since morning — not grey but a deep, bruised orange that spread along the edge of the sky the way rust spreads at the hem of something left too long in the wet. My neighbour appeared at the back fence the way she sometimes did when she had something to say. She had been through the 2009 fires in Victoria and carried that knowledge without drama. 'Go now,' she said. 'Don't take the highway.' She did not elaborate and I did not ask her to. I put the bag in the car, called the dog, and left. The house was still standing when I came back four days later. I have thought about what she said many times since — not only in the context of fires.

Extract C

Fire is not a substance but a process: the rapid oxidation of a combustible material in the presence of heat and oxygen, releasing light, heat, and combustion gases as by-products. Fire scientists describe the conditions necessary for combustion using the concept of the 'fire triangle': fuel, oxygen, and heat must all be present simultaneously for a fire to ignite and continue. Remove any one of these elements — smother the flames to cut off oxygen, drench the fuel with water to lower its temperature below its ignition point, or eliminate the combustible material entirely — and the fire will cease. The temperature at which a given material begins to release flammable vapours is known as its 'pyrolysis temperature', a figure that varies significantly between substances.

When fire burns in an enclosed space, it can produce two particularly dangerous phenomena that investigators and firefighters are trained to anticipate. The first is 'flashover': the point at which radiant heat raises every combustible surface in a room simultaneously to its ignition temperature, causing the entire contents of the space to ignite at once. The second is 'backdraft': the explosive ignition that occurs when a fire that has been starved of oxygen suddenly receives a fresh supply — typically when a door or window is opened. Researchers in fire dynamics also study 'fire plume behaviour' — the patterns by which hot gases and smoke rise and spread through a structure — in order to model how a fire is likely to develop in complex environments.

Extract D

For Aboriginal Australians, fire was not a force to be avoided but a tool to be applied with precision and purpose. Across most of the continent, Aboriginal communities practised what is now called 'cultural burning' — the deliberate, low-intensity burning of vegetation at carefully chosen times and places to maintain the health of Country. These burns served multiple purposes simultaneously: they cleared accumulated dry growth that would otherwise fuel a high-intensity wildfire, encouraged the regeneration of food plants and the habitat of animals that communities depended on, and kept pathways open across the landscape. The knowledge required to burn safely and effectively — which plants to burn, when, in what direction, and under what weather conditions — was accumulated over tens of thousands of years and passed between generations through oral tradition, ceremony, and direct practice.

Following European colonisation, cultural burning was actively suppressed in many parts of Australia, partly through legislation that restricted the lighting of fires on land that had been taken from Aboriginal communities, and partly through a wider dismissal of Indigenous land management as primitive or dangerous. The consequences of this suppression became increasingly apparent over the twentieth century, as the fuel loads that cultural burning had historically kept in check were allowed to accumulate unchecked, contributing to the conditions that enabled catastrophic wildfires. In recent decades, land management authorities in several states have begun working with Aboriginal communities to reintegrate cultural burning into broader fire management strategies, in a formal recognition that this knowledge represents one of the most effective and ecologically sound approaches to fire stewardship ever developed.
