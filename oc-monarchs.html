The Migration of Monarch Butterflies

Each autumn, something extraordinary happens across the forests and meadows of North America. Hundreds of millions of monarch butterflies abandon their summer feeding grounds and set off on a journey of up to four thousand kilometres, converging on a small cluster of forests in the mountains of central Mexico. They have never made this journey before. The forest where they are headed is one they have never seen. Yet, with remarkable consistency, they arrive.

For decades, the destination of North America's monarch butterflies was one of the great unsolved mysteries of the natural world. The zoologist Dr Fred Urquhart dedicated much of his career to finding it. Working alongside him was his wife Norah, whose contribution to the search proved decisive. 12 ..................... By 1975, more than three thousand volunteers across North America were participating in the tagging programme, and reports were arriving from every corner of the continent. It was one of these reports, from a volunteer in Mexico, that finally led the Urquharts to the overwintering site in 1976: a mountainside in Michoacán, blanketed in orange and black.

13 ..................... What researchers have since discovered about the monarch's navigation system has forced them to reconsider that assumption entirely. Scientists have established that monarchs use the position of the sun as their primary compass, relying on what is known as a time-compensated sun compass, built around an internal clock located in their antennae. 14 ..................... Researchers have also found evidence that monarchs are sensitive to the Earth's magnetic field, which may provide a back-up system when cloud cover makes the sun unavailable. 15 ..................... The problem, researchers note, is not merely theoretical. Monarchs that are artificially displaced hundreds of kilometres from their usual route still manage to correct their course and arrive at the overwintering site in Michoacán.

Over four decades of tagging data, collected from hundreds of thousands of individual butterflies, have revealed patterns that continue to surprise researchers. 16 ..................... One butterfly, tagged in Ontario and recovered seven months later in a forest in Michoacán, had covered a distance of more than four thousand kilometres — a journey that researchers described as, mile for mile, equivalent to a human being walking from London to the surface of the Moon. 17 .....................

The monarch's migration is now considered one of the most endangered natural phenomena on Earth. Milkweed, the only plant on which monarchs lay their eggs, has declined sharply across North America as agricultural land has been treated with herbicides. The forests of Michoacán, where the butterflies overwinter, are threatened by illegal logging. The journey continues, for now. Whether it will do so indefinitely remains uncertain.
