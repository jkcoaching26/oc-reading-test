Extracts: Country

Extract A

I left the town where I grew up at eighteen and told myself I had outgrown it. For twelve years I came back only when I had to — Christmas, a wedding, and now this. The paddocks along the highway had the same dry patience I remembered; the silos were still painted with the same faded mural of wheat. The main street had lost two shops and gained one. Almost nothing had changed.

I had expected this to feel like returning. It felt more like being watched. The place seemed to remember me in a way I had not prepared for, and I found myself moving through it carefully, the way you move through a room where something has been left exactly as it was.

At the funeral, an older woman I barely recognised said: 'You were always going to come back.' I had been about to correct her. But I stopped, because I was not completely sure she was wrong. I had spent twelve years building a life somewhere else, and the town had waited with what I could only describe as patience. Whether that was a comfort or a warning was something I still had not worked out by the time I drove back to the highway.

Extract B

The countryside is not disappearing. It is being replaced by something that looks like it. Across parts of Britain, Europe and Australia, a movement known as rewilding is returning degraded farmland to native ecosystems — removing fences, reintroducing lost species, and allowing land that has been cropped or grazed for generations to revert. What emerges looks wild. In many cases, it is the most 'natural' landscape those regions have seen in centuries.

The paradox is that what people picture when they imagine the natural countryside — rolling hills, hedgerows, open pasture — is itself a product of centuries of human management. The ancient forests that once covered much of these regions were cleared long ago. The picturesque landscapes now considered worth preserving were shaped by ploughs, sheep and drainage channels, not left by nature alone.

Rewilding does not restore an original state because no single original state exists. It creates something new from the pieces that remain. Scientists working in this field are increasingly comfortable with this idea: that nature is not a fixed condition to be returned to, but a process that can be restarted wherever people choose to step back.

Extract C

In the past decade, moving from the city to a rural town has become something many Australians speak of with genuine longing. They want space, quiet, a slower pace, a connection to the land that city life does not offer. The number of people actually making the move has grown steadily, and the towns receiving them have seen real changes as a result.

What those towns have also seen is the arrival of people who bring their expectations with them. Old buildings are renovated in ways that suit weekend visitors more than working families. Land prices rise. Farmers' markets replace hardware stores. The character that made a place appealing is gradually altered by the desire to preserve it. The newcomers, almost without exception, describe themselves as the ones who truly understand what the country is for.

The people who have lived in these towns for generations are rarely asked. They are, in many cases, still there — watching the transformation of the place they grew up in by people who moved there precisely because it had not yet been transformed. There is a particular kind of enthusiasm for the country that tends to leave it worse off than it found it.

Extract D

In many Aboriginal Australian cultures, the word Country does not mean what the English word suggests. It is not a place you come from or a landscape you observe. Country is a living relationship — something that holds you as much as you hold it. To be 'on Country' is not simply to be standing on a piece of land. It is to be within a web of responsibilities, stories, and connections that extend to waterways, sky, plants, animals, and the generations who came before.

This understanding makes ownership, in the European legal sense, a difficult concept to apply. You cannot own a relationship. You can belong to a place; the place can belong to you; but these are not the same as the titles and boundaries that European law uses to describe land. For many Aboriginal peoples, the idea that land could be sold — transferred from one set of relationships to another — was not merely wrong. It was, in a fundamental sense, incomprehensible.

Land management authorities in several Australian states have begun incorporating this understanding into their work. Caring for Country programs, developed in partnership with Traditional Custodians, are now used to maintain ecosystems, manage fire, and protect waterways in ways that combine scientific knowledge with generations of accumulated practice.
