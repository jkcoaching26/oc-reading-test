Extract A

We had been at sea for four days when the whale appeared. Not dramatically — no breach, no spray — just a long grey shape that surfaced about twenty metres from the hull and stayed there, moving at our speed. I had seen whales before, from a distance, through binoculars. This was different. This was adjacency.

I stood at the rail for a long time without moving. The whale did not seem interested in us exactly, but neither did it leave. At one point it rolled slightly to the side, and I saw the eye — a dark disc, larger than my fist, pointed in my direction. I am aware that this is the sort of moment people over-narrate, loading it with significance it may not have had. But I am also certain that something shifted in me that afternoon: not an emotion I could name, but a rearrangement of some assumption I had not known I was carrying.

I thought about it for the rest of the voyage. The whale, I kept thinking, had no reason to stay. It was not trapped, not injured, not attracted by food. Whatever brought it alongside us that afternoon, it was not need. That seemed important, though I could not have said why.

Extract B

Silence, in the ocean, is not the absence of sound. It is the absence of us. Before propeller-driven ships, before sonar, before the constant percussion of offshore construction, the sea was a place of extraordinary acoustic richness. Whales sang to each other across ocean basins. Shrimp produced snapping sounds dense enough to disorient fish. Reefs generated a constant, intricate hum that juvenile fish used as a homing signal to find their way back from open water.

Human activity has steadily overwritten this soundscape. Shipping lanes now cross the migration routes of blue whales, whose low-frequency calls — evolved to carry across thousands of kilometres — are being masked by engine noise at exactly the frequencies they use to locate one another. Some populations have responded by shifting their songs to higher pitches; others have simply gone quiet. For species that rely on sound the way sighted animals rely on vision, this is not a minor inconvenience. It is a form of blindness.

Unlike plastic waste or oil spills, noise pollution leaves no visible trace. It cannot be photographed or filmed in ways that produce outrage. Yet researchers tracking whale behaviour in busy shipping corridors have documented increased stress hormones, changes in feeding patterns, and in some cases, strandings that appear linked to acoustic disruption. The ocean's voice is being steadily erased, and most of the world has not yet noticed.

Extract C

There has never been a better time to love nature from a safe distance. Wildlife documentaries now offer footage of such technical brilliance — slow-motion predator strikes, time-lapse coral blooms, night-vision footage of creatures no field researcher has observed in decades — that the natural world can feel more vivid on screen than it does outside. Audiences watch in their millions, and the response, consistently, is wonder.

What is less often noted is what this wonder does not produce. Studies tracking the behaviour of documentary audiences find that emotional engagement with nature on screen correlates poorly with environmentally protective behaviour in daily life. Feeling moved, it turns out, is not the same as being moved to act. The documentary format has become extraordinarily good at generating concern, and rather less good at translating that concern into anything that might actually help. We have, in effect, learned to experience the natural world as something that happens to other species in other places, while we watch, care deeply, and carry on.

This is not an argument against wildlife filmmaking, which has done more than almost any other medium to bring public attention to endangered ecosystems. But there is a difference between awareness and action, and the gap between the two — comfortable, well-lit, and emotionally satisfying — is precisely where the natural world is losing ground.

Extract D

In 2005, the journalist and author Richard Louv coined the term 'nature deficit disorder' to describe what he saw as a growing disconnection between human beings and the natural world — particularly among children raised in urban environments with limited access to unstructured outdoor time. The phrase was not a clinical diagnosis but a cultural provocation, and it succeeded: it gave a name to something many people recognised without having articulated.

The research that has followed gives the provocation some weight. Studies across multiple countries have found that time spent in natural environments — forests, parks, coastlines, even gardens — produces measurable reductions in cortisol levels, heart rate, and self-reported anxiety, effects that screen-based relaxation does not reliably replicate. Something about direct contact with living, uncontrolled environments appears to act on the nervous system in ways that designed, predictable spaces do not. Researchers have begun calling this effect 'restoration,' and they are increasingly certain it requires actual nature, not a simulation of it.

Some cities have begun to take this seriously. Architects working in what is known as biophilic design are incorporating living walls, natural light, water features, and roof gardens into buildings that would previously have contained none of these things. The argument is not aesthetic but physiological: if the environments we inhabit shape how we feel, then redesigning those environments is one of the most practical interventions available. Nature, on this account, is not a luxury to be visited. It is an infrastructure to be built in.
